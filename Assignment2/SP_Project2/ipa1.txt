<p><b>India pale ale</b> (<b>IPA</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hoppy</a> <a href="/wiki/Beer_styles" title="Beer styles" class="mw-redirect">beer style</a> within the broader category of <a href="/wiki/Pale_ale" title="Pale ale">pale ale</a>.</p>
<p>The first known use of the term "India pale ale" is an advertisement in the <i><a href="/wiki/Sydney_Gazette" title="Sydney Gazette">Sydney Gazette and New South Wales Advertiser</a></i> in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was also referred to as <i>pale ale as prepared for India</i>, <i>India ale</i>, <i>pale India ale</i>, or <i>pale export India ale</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=India_pale_ale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>pale ale</i> originally denoted an ale that had been brewed from pale <a href="/wiki/Malt" title="Malt">malt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The pale ales of the early 18th century were lightly <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hopped</a> and quite different from today's pale ales.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (February 2015)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> By the mid-18th century, pale ale was mostly brewed with <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>-fired malt, which produced less smoking and roasting of <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> in the malting process, and hence produced a paler beer.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One such variety of beer was <a href="/wiki/Old_ale" title="Old ale">October beer</a>, a pale well-hopped brew popular among the <a href="/wiki/Landed_gentry" title="Landed gentry">landed classes</a>, who brewed it domestically; once brewed it was intended to <a href="/wiki/Wine_cellar" title="Wine cellar">cellar</a> two years.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg/280px-Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg" width="280" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg/420px-Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg/560px-Old_Phipps_IPA_Claret_sharpened.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="707" /></a>

<p>Among the first brewers known to export beer to India was the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bow_Brewery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bow Brewery (page does not exist)">Bow Brewery</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>-<a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> border. Bow Brewery beers became popular among <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a> traders in the late 18th century because of the brewery's location<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup> and Hodgson's liberal <a href="/wiki/Line_of_credit" title="Line of credit">credit line</a> of 18 months. Ships transported Hodgson's beers to India, among them his October beer, which benefited exceptionally from conditions of the voyage and was apparently highly regarded among its consumers in India.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Bow Brewery came into the control of Hodgson's son in the early 19th century,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[b]</a></sup> but his business practices alienated their customers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During the same period, several <a href="/wiki/Burton_upon_Trent" title="Burton upon Trent">Burton</a> breweries lost their European export market in Russia when the <a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar</a> banned the trade, and were seeking a new export market for their beer.<sup id="cite_ref-Pryor2009_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pryor2009-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the behest of the East India Company, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Allsopp_%26_Sons" title="Samuel Allsopp &amp; Sons">Allsopp</a> brewery developed a strongly-hopped pale ale in the style of Hodgson's for export to India.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> Other Burton brewers, including <a href="/wiki/Bass_Brewery" title="Bass Brewery">Bass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salt%27s_Brewery" title="Salt's Brewery" class="mw-redirect">Salt</a>, were eager to replace their lost Russian export market and quickly followed Allsopp's lead. Perhaps as a result of the advantages of Burton water in brewing,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[c]</a></sup> Burton India pale ale was preferred by merchants and their customers in India, but Hodgson's October beer clearly influenced the Burton brewers' India pale ales.</p>
<p>Brewer <a href="/wiki/Charrington_Brewery" title="Charrington Brewery">Charrington</a>'s trial shipments of <a href="/wiki/Hogshead" title="Hogshead">hogsheads</a> of "India Ale" to Madras (now <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>) and Calcutta (now <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>) in 1827 proved successful and a regular trade emerged with the key British agents and retailers: Griffiths &amp; Co in Madras; Adam, Skinner and Co. in Bombay (now <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>) and Bruce, Allen &amp; Co. in Calcutta.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMathias1959190_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMathias1959190-15">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early IPA, such as Burton brewers' and Hodgson's, was only slightly higher in alcohol than most beer brewed in his day and would not have been considered a strong ale; however, a greater proportion of the <a href="/wiki/Wort" title="Wort">wort</a> was well-fermented, leaving behind few residual sugars, and the beer was strongly hopped.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[13]</a></sup> The common story that early IPAs were much stronger than other beers of the time, however, is a myth.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[14]</a></sup> While IPAs were formulated to survive long voyages by sea better than other styles of the time, <a href="/wiki/Porter_(beer)" title="Porter (beer)">porter</a> was also shipped to India and California successfully.<sup id="cite_ref-AnchorIPA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AnchorIPA-18">[15]</a></sup> It is clear that by the 1860s, India pale ales were widely brewed in England, and that they were much more <a href="/wiki/Attenuation_(brewing)" title="Attenuation (brewing)">attenuated</a> and highly hopped than porters and many other ales.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Demand for the export style of pale ale, which had become known as <i>India pale ale</i>, developed in England around 1840 and India pale ale became a popular product in England.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[18]</a></sup> Some brewers dropped the term "India" in the late 19th century, but records indicated that these "pale ales" retained the features of earlier IPAs.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[19]</a></sup> American, Australian, and Canadian brewers manufactured beer with the label IPA before 1900, and records suggest that these beers were similar to English IPA of the era.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>IPA style beers started being exported to other colonial countries, such as Australia and New Zealand, around this time with many breweries dropping the 'I' in 'IPA' and simply calling them Pale Ales or Export Pales. Many breweries, such as <a href="/wiki/Kirkstall_Brewery" title="Kirkstall Brewery">Kirkstall Brewery</a>, sent large quantities of export beer across the world by steam ship to auction off to wholesalers once there.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=India_pale_ale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term IPA is commonly used in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> for <a href="/wiki/Gravity_(alcoholic_beverage)" title="Gravity (alcoholic beverage)">low-gravity</a> beers, for example <a href="/wiki/Greene_King" title="Greene King">Greene King</a> IPA and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wells_Ltd" title="Charles Wells Ltd">Charles Wells</a> Eagle IPA. IPAs with <a href="/wiki/Abv" title="Abv" class="mw-redirect">alcohol by volume</a> of 4% or lower have been brewed in the UK since the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War" class="mw-redirect">First World War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[22]</a></sup> when taxes on beer ingredients greatly increased and brewers responded by lowering the strength of their beers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada_and_the_United_States">Canada and the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=India_pale_ale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Canada and the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>IPAs have a long history in Canada and the United States, and many breweries there produce a version of the style.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[23]</a></sup> Contemporary American IPAs are typically brewed with distinctively American hops, such as <a href="/wiki/Cascade_hop" title="Cascade hop">Cascade</a>, Centennial, Citra, Columbus, <a href="/wiki/Chinook_hops" title="Chinook hops" class="mw-redirect">Chinook</a>, Simcoe, <a href="/wiki/Amarillo_hops" title="Amarillo hops">Amarillo</a>, Tomahawk, Warrior, and Nugget.</p>
<p>East Coast IPAs are distinguished from West Coast IPAs by a stronger malt presence, which balances the intensity of the hops, whereas hops are more prominent in the western brews, possibly because of the proximity of West Coast breweries to hop fields in the Pacific Northwest. East Coast breweries rely more on spicier European hops and specialty malts than those on the West Coast.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Double IPAs (also referred to as Imperial IPAs) are a stronger, very hoppy variant of IPAs that typically have alcohol content above 7.5% by volume.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[27]</a></sup> The style is claimed to have originated with Vinnie Cilurzo, currently the owner of <a href="/wiki/Russian_River_Brewing_Company" title="Russian River Brewing Company">Russian River Brewing Company</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa,_California" title="Santa Rosa, California">Santa Rosa, California</a>, in 1994 at the now-defunct Blind Pig Brewery in <a href="/wiki/Temecula,_California" title="Temecula, California">Temecula, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[d]</a></sup> The style has been embraced by the <a href="/wiki/Beer_in_San_Diego_County,_California" title="Beer in San Diego County, California">craft brewers</a> of <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_County,_California" title="San Diego County, California">San Diego County, California</a>, to such an extent that double IPAs have been referred to as "San Diego pale ale".<sup id="cite_ref-renamed_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-renamed-32">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rocked_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rocked-33">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, IPAs have become extremely popular, helping drive the craft beer renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[30]</a></sup> The style has traditionally been known for being higher in alcohol content (<a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">ABV</a>), but in recent years, "<a href="/wiki/Session_beer" title="Session beer" class="mw-redirect">session</a>" IPAs lower in ABV have become popular. As the style has grown more popular, many sub-styles have emerged, such as Belgian or White IPAs.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>American-style IPA is now brewed in <a href="/wiki/Beer_in_Belgium" title="Beer in Belgium">Belgium</a>, with Viven IPA from <a href="/wiki/De_Proefbrouwerij" title="De Proefbrouwerij">De Proefbrouwerij</a> and Houblon <a href="/wiki/Chouffe" title="Chouffe" class="mw-redirect">Chouffe</a> becoming available in the 2000s. It is also available in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> from Cervecería Antares.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[32]</a></sup></p>
